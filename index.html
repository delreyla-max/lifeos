        🔎 Deeper analysis — income drivers since 2010 · variance decomposition

        The long arc: 2010 → 2023

        Total income fell from $198K (2010) to a $29K trough (2018), then recovered to a record $181K (2023). The 2010-13 drop was a wage collapse ($192K → $32K W-2) only partly offset by a self-employment push (Schedule C peaked at $56,887 in 2013 — sole-proprietor income; business name isn't on the parsed PDF). The recovery since 2019 is a different mix: W-2 wages climbing back ($99K → $131K) plus property and rental income that didn't exist in the wage years.

        Biggest single swing: 2023 vs 2022 (+$71,972)
        2022 total income$108,655
        ＋ Capital gains (Big Bear sale)+$37,849
        ＋ Rental swing to positive+$19,815
        ＋ Wage growth+$11,457
        ＋ Dividends/interest+$2,851

        What it means: income is getting less wage-dependent. In 2010, ~97% was W-2 wages; in 2023, wages were ~73% and the rest came from capital gains, rental, and dividends. The volatility now lives in one-time property sales (2019, 2023) — strip those out and the underlying earned income is ~$130-145K. Effective tax rate tracks those sale years, so timing the next sale (or spreading it) is the main tax lever.
